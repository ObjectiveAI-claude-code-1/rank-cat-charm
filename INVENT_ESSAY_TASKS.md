1. Evaluate the emotional resonance of each cat picture. Determine whether the image provokes a genuine, involuntary emotional response — warmth, humor, tenderness, delight, or quiet melancholy. A picture with strong emotional resonance makes the viewer react before they think. It creates a real feeling, not a manufactured one. Technically beautiful but emotionally inert images should score low on this dimension, while even imperfect snapshots that capture an undeniable moment of feeling should score high.

2. Evaluate the narrative character of each cat picture. Determine whether the image reveals the individual personality of the cat and suggests a story unfolding around it. The strongest images feel like a single frame pulled from a larger life — you sense the before and after, and you understand who this cat is. Look for images where the cat's nature comes through clearly — curiosity in the tilt of a head, mischief in wide eyes beside a knocked-over glass, dignity in a perfectly composed posture — and where the environment or context enriches the micro-narrative. Images that feel anonymous, where the cat could be any cat and the moment could be any moment, should score low.

3. Evaluate the magnetic presence of each cat picture. Determine whether the image has the raw ability to capture and hold attention — the quality that makes a viewer stop scrolling, look longer, and want to share it with someone. This is the hardest quality to articulate but the most instinctive to recognize. It is the sense that an image is alive and pulling you toward it, whether through dramatic intensity, perfect comedic timing, or unexpected tenderness. Images that are passively pleasant but lack the pull to make someone stop should score low, while images that actively demand a reaction should score high.
